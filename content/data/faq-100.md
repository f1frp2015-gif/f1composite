# 100 Most Frequently Asked Questions About Pultruded FRP Profiles

Based on industry standards (EN 13706, ASTM D3917, ASCE/SEI 74-23, ISO 14125), published technical references, and F1 Composite engineering data.

---

## Engineers (25 Questions)

**Q1: How does the strength-to-weight ratio of pultruded FRP compare to steel?**
A: Glass FRP has a tensile strength of approximately 207-414 MPa at a density of 1.8 g/cm3, while A709 Grade 50 steel has 345 MPa tensile strength at 7.8 g/cm3. Pound for pound, GFRP is stronger than steel in tension, though steel has higher absolute stiffness (E = 200 GPa vs. 20-40 GPa for GFRP). Design of FRP members is typically governed by serviceability (deflection) rather than ultimate strength.

**Q2: What design codes and standards apply to pultruded FRP structural profiles?**
A: The primary standards are EN 13706 (Parts 1-3) for European markets, which classifies profiles into grades E17 and E23 based on flexural modulus. In North America, ASCE Pre-Standard for LRFD of Pultruded FRP Structures, ASTM D3917 (dimensional tolerances), and ASTM D7290 (statistical basis for design) apply. ACI 440.1R covers FRP reinforcement. The Eurocomp Design Code and manufacturer design manuals (e.g., Strongwell, Fiberline) provide supplementary guidance.

**Q3: How do I calculate deflection for pultruded FRP beams?**
A: Use Timoshenko beam theory, which accounts for shear deformation -- critical for FRP due to low shear modulus (typically 3-4 GPa). Total deflection = bending deflection + shear deflection. For a simply supported beam under uniform load: delta = 5wL^4/(384EI) + wL^2/(8kAG), where k is the shear correction factor (~0.5 for I-sections). Always use the full-section flexural modulus from testing per EN 13706-2, not coupon-level data.

**Q4: How does creep affect long-term FRP structural design?**
A: Pultruded GFRP exhibits viscoelastic creep under sustained load. Design practice limits sustained stress to 20-25% of ultimate strength to prevent creep rupture (per ACI 440.1R). A creep reduction factor of 0.3-0.4 is typically applied to the elastic modulus for long-term deflection calculations. Findley's power law model is commonly used to predict time-dependent deformation. Creep is more pronounced at elevated temperatures (above 65 deg C).

**Q5: What is the buckling behavior of pultruded FRP columns and how is it designed?**
A: FRP profiles are prone to local and global buckling due to their high strength-to-stiffness ratio and thin-walled geometry. Local flange buckling, web buckling, and lateral-torsional buckling must all be checked. Use the ASCE Pre-Standard or Eurocomp methods, which adapt classical plate buckling theory with orthotropic elastic constants. Critical buckling loads are strongly influenced by the ratio of longitudinal to transverse modulus (typically 3:1 for standard pultrusions).

**Q6: What fire ratings can pultruded FRP profiles achieve?**
A: Standard polyester FRP achieves Class 1 surface spread of flame per BS 476 Part 7, with a flame spread index of 15-25 per ASTM E84. Fire-retardant (FR) resins with brominated or phosphorus-based additives can achieve Class A (FSI less than 25, SDI less than 450). However, FRP softens above its glass transition temperature (Tg, typically 100-150 deg C for vinyl ester), so structural capacity degrades in fire. Intumescent coatings or fire-retardant resin formulations are used where enhanced fire performance is required.

**Q7: How do I design bolted connections in pultruded FRP?**
A: FRP connections fail by bearing, net-tension, shear-out, or cleavage -- not by bolt shear as in steel. Minimum edge distance is 4x bolt diameter; minimum bolt spacing is 4-5x diameter. Use stainless steel or FRP bolts with large washers to distribute bearing stress. The ASCE Pre-Standard and Eurocomp provide bearing strength equations. Torque should be limited (typically 20-30 Nm for M12 bolts) to prevent through-thickness crushing. Connection efficiency is typically 40-60% of member capacity.

**Q8: Can pultruded FRP be used in seismic zones?**
A: Yes. FRP's low mass (75-80% lighter than steel) significantly reduces seismic inertial forces on the structure. Topglass and others have published design guidelines for FRP in seismic zones. However, FRP connections are typically semi-rigid and lack ductility, so energy dissipation must be provided through connection detailing or supplementary damping devices. FRP cladding and secondary structures are particularly advantageous in seismic retrofit applications.

**Q9: What is the fatigue performance of pultruded FRP?**
A: Pultruded GFRP retains 50-60% of its static strength after 10 million load cycles at an R-ratio of 0.1, making it superior to welded steel details. Fatigue design typically uses an S-N curve approach with a fatigue-life exponent of ~10 (vs. ~3 for steel). Vinylester-based profiles outperform polyester in fatigue. Bolted connections are the weakest link; adhesively bonded joints offer better fatigue resistance. ASTM D3479 provides the standard test method.

**Q10: How does FRP perform in corrosive chemical environments?**
A: GFRP with vinylester resin resists most acids (pH 1-14), salts, solvents, and oxidizing agents. It is the standard choice for chemical plants, wastewater treatment, and offshore platforms. Salt fog testing per ASTM B117 shows less than 1% mass loss after 10,000 hours. However, strong alkalis above 30% concentration and certain oxidizing acids (e.g., concentrated HNO3) can degrade glass fibers. ECR-glass reinforcement provides enhanced alkaline resistance. Always verify resin compatibility with specific chemicals using the manufacturer's corrosion guide.

**Q11: What is the elastic modulus of pultruded FRP in the transverse direction?**
A: Pultruded profiles are orthotropic: the longitudinal modulus (direction of pultrusion) is typically 20-40 GPa, while the transverse modulus is only 5-10 GPa (roughly 25-30% of longitudinal). This anisotropy directly affects lateral load distribution, Poisson effects, and connection design. EN 13706-3 Grade E23 requires a minimum full-section longitudinal flexural modulus of 23 GPa. Designers must use direction-dependent properties in all calculations.

**Q12: How do I specify FRP profiles for outdoor UV exposure?**
A: Require a UV-stabilized polyester or vinylester resin system with a minimum polyester surface veil (30-50 g/m2) on all exposed faces. The veil protects structural fibers from UV degradation and moisture ingress. Color-stable pigments and UV inhibitors are added to the resin. Per ASTM G154 accelerated weathering tests, well-formulated FRP retains more than 90% of flexural strength after 10,000 hours of UV exposure. Specify compliance with ASTM D4385 for visual surface quality.

**Q13: What is the coefficient of thermal expansion of pultruded FRP?**
A: The longitudinal CTE is 6-10 x 10^-6 /deg C (similar to concrete and half that of aluminum). The transverse CTE is higher, approximately 18-25 x 10^-6 /deg C, due to resin-dominated behavior. This anisotropy must be considered in long runs of FRP profiles. Thermal movement joints are recommended every 12-15 meters for continuous profiles. FRP's low thermal conductivity (0.2-0.4 W/m.K) makes it an excellent thermal break material.

**Q14: Can pultruded FRP profiles be used as primary load-bearing structural members?**
A: Yes. Pultruded wide-flange beams, channels, and box sections are used as primary beams, columns, and truss members in buildings, bridges, and industrial structures. The Fiberline bridge in Denmark and the Aberfeldy footbridge in Scotland are landmark examples. Design typically follows the ASCE Pre-Standard or Eurocomp, with safety factors of 2.5-4.0 on material strength depending on limit state and load duration.

**Q15: How does temperature affect the mechanical properties of pultruded FRP?**
A: Mechanical properties begin to degrade above 65-80 deg C as the resin approaches its glass transition temperature (Tg). Standard polyester Tg is 100-120 deg C; vinylester is 120-150 deg C; polyurethane is typically 130-160 deg C. At Tg, stiffness drops by 40-60%. Below -40 deg C, FRP becomes more brittle but retains its strength. Strongwell's design manual provides property adjustment charts for temperatures from -60 deg F to 300 deg F. Always specify resin Tg at least 30 deg C above maximum service temperature.

**Q16: What are the standard pultruded profile shapes available?**
A: Standard catalog shapes include I/H-beams (76-305 mm deep), channels (76-305 mm), angles (equal and unequal, 25-152 mm), square and rectangular tubes (25-203 mm), round tubes (25-152 mm), flat bars, solid rods, and plates. Wide-flange and narrow-flange variants are available. Fiberline, Strongwell (EXTREN), and Bedford (PROForms) maintain the largest standard catalogs. Custom die profiles are available with typical tooling lead times of 6-10 weeks.

**Q17: How do I account for the anisotropic behavior of FRP in finite element analysis?**
A: Model FRP as an orthotropic material with independent elastic constants: E_L (longitudinal), E_T (transverse), G_LT (in-plane shear), and nu_LT (Poisson's ratio). Use shell elements for thin-walled profiles. Typical values: E_L = 23-30 GPa, E_T = 7-10 GPa, G_LT = 3-4 GPa, nu_LT = 0.28-0.33. The Tsai-Hill or maximum stress failure criterion is commonly used. Layer-by-layer analysis using classical lamination theory gives the most accurate results for custom layups.

**Q18: What is the impact resistance of pultruded FRP compared to steel?**
A: FRP absorbs impact energy through matrix cracking and fiber pullout rather than plastic deformation. Impact energy absorption is lower than mild steel but can be improved by using vinylester resin and woven reinforcements. For guardrail and handrail applications, pultruded sections typically pass a 100 kg soft-body pendulum impact test. FRP does not deform permanently after moderate impacts -- it either survives undamaged or fractures, with no hidden yielding.

**Q19: Can FRP profiles be used in combination with concrete (composite construction)?**
A: Yes. FRP profiles serve as permanent formwork and tension reinforcement in FRP-concrete hybrid beams, combining FRP's tensile strength and corrosion resistance with concrete's compressive strength and stiffness. This approach overcomes FRP's low stiffness limitation. Research at the University of Warwick and others has shown 30-50% stiffness improvements over FRP-only sections. ASTM D7205 covers tensile testing of FRP bars used as concrete reinforcement.

**Q20: What are the design considerations for FRP in marine environments?**
A: Marine applications require vinylester or isophthalic polyester resin for resistance to saltwater, chlorides, and marine organisms. Specify ECR-glass or corrosion-grade glass fibers. UV-stabilized gel coat or surface veil is essential for above-waterline elements. Pultruded FRP has been proven in offshore platforms with 30+ year service life. It eliminates galvanic corrosion when adjacent to metals. DNV-OS-C501 and Lloyd's Register guidelines provide design rules for marine composite structures.

**Q21: How do I compare life-cycle cost of FRP versus steel structures?**
A: While initial material cost of FRP is typically 1.5-3x that of steel, total life-cycle cost is typically 20-40% lower in corrosive environments. FRP eliminates painting (steel repainting costs $15-30/m2 every 7-10 years), requires no cathodic protection, has zero downtime for corrosion repairs, and weighs 75% less (reducing foundation and installation costs). A typical 25-year LCC analysis for a chemical plant walkway shows FRP at 60-70% of the cost of hot-dip galvanized steel.

**Q22: What is the durability of FRP in alkaline environments (concrete contact)?**
A: Standard E-glass in polyester resin degrades when in prolonged contact with alkaline solutions (pH > 12.5). For concrete embedment or splash zones, specify ECR-glass fibers with vinylester resin, which are immune to alkaline attack per ASTM D578. AR-glass (alkali-resistant glass) is another option. Long-term durability has been proven in infrastructure applications exceeding 20 years. CSA S807 and ACI 440.1R provide environmental reduction factors (0.7-0.8) for alkaline exposure.

**Q23: What testing is required to qualify pultruded FRP for structural use?**
A: At minimum: full-section flexural test (EN 13706-2), tensile test (ISO 527/ASTM D638), compressive test (ASTM D695), interlaminar shear (ASTM D2344), bearing strength (ASTM D953), and pin-bearing (ASTM D953). EN 13706-3 defines two quality grades (E17 and E23) with specific minimum values. Fire testing per ASTM E84 or BS 476 is typically required for building applications. A minimum of 5 specimens per test with statistical analysis per ASTM D7290 is best practice.

**Q24: How do I design FRP profiles for concentrated loads and point loads?**
A: The low transverse stiffness of pultruded profiles means concentrated loads can cause web crippling and flange local bending. Use stiffening plates, gussets, or saddle-type load spreaders at point load locations. Web crippling capacity is typically 5-15 kN for standard I-sections. The Eurocomp design code provides web crippling checks. For equipment support, use FRP bearing plates with a minimum bearing area to keep contact stress below 30-40 MPa.

**Q25: What is the Poisson's ratio and shear modulus of pultruded GFRP?**
A: The major Poisson's ratio (nu_LT) is typically 0.28-0.33 and the minor Poisson's ratio (nu_TL) is 0.10-0.12, reflecting the orthotropic nature. In-plane shear modulus (G_LT) is 3-4 GPa, roughly 7-10x lower than steel (80 GPa). This low shear modulus means shear deformation is significant in short-span beams and must be included in deflection calculations. Use Timoshenko beam theory or ensure span-to-depth ratios exceed 20:1 for Euler-Bernoulli theory to be sufficiently accurate.

## Architects (15 Questions)

**Q26: What colors are available for pultruded FRP profiles?**
A: Standard catalog colors typically include gray, green, yellow, beige, white, and dark gray. Custom RAL or Pantone color matching is available from most manufacturers by adding pigments to the resin during pultrusion. Dark colors absorb more heat and may experience higher surface temperatures; for exterior applications in hot climates, lighter colors or high-Tg resins are recommended. Color is integral (throughout the cross-section), so minor scratches do not reveal a different substrate.

**Q27: What surface finishes are available for pultruded FRP?**
A: Standard pultrusion produces a smooth, low-gloss finish. Options include: UV-resistant gel coat for high-gloss exterior surfaces, textured/anti-slip surfaces for walkway applications, painted finish using two-part polyurethane coatings for exact color/gloss matching, sandblasted finish for improved adhesive bonding, and wood-grain embossed finishes for architectural applications. Surface veil (polyester or glass) provides a resin-rich exterior that protects fibers and improves UV resistance.

**Q28: How does the appearance of FRP weather over time outdoors?**
A: With proper UV-stabilized resin and surface veil, FRP profiles maintain their appearance for 20-30 years outdoors. Without UV protection, surfaces may exhibit slight chalking (whitish powder) and color fading within 5-10 years, but structural properties are unaffected. UV degradation is limited to the outer 0.1-0.3 mm. Regular cleaning with mild detergent restores appearance. Fiberline offers a 20-year warranty against structural degradation from weathering.

**Q29: What profile shapes can be custom-designed for architectural projects?**
A: Pultrusion can produce virtually any constant cross-section profile, including complex hollow sections, multi-cell shapes, integrated channels for wiring, snap-fit connections, and decorative moldings. Custom die tooling costs $5,000-$25,000 depending on complexity, with lead times of 6-10 weeks. Minimum production runs for custom profiles are typically 500-2,000 meters. Fiberline, Exel Composites, and Strongwell all offer custom profile design services with in-house engineering support.

**Q30: How does FRP contribute to sustainable/green building and LEED certification?**
A: FRP can contribute to LEED credits in several categories: reduced transportation energy (75% lighter than steel), extended service life reducing replacement cycles, lower embodied energy over full lifecycle, regional manufacturing credits, and heat island reduction with light-colored profiles. FRP manufacturing produces 60-70% less CO2 per ton compared to steel. The 50-100+ year lifespan means fewer replacements. However, end-of-life recyclability remains limited compared to steel and aluminum.

**Q31: Is pultruded FRP recyclable?**
A: Thermoset FRP (polyester/vinylester) cannot be re-melted like metals. Mechanical recycling (grinding into filler material) is commercially available but yields low-value products. Chemical recycling (solvolysis) and thermal recycling (pyrolysis, cement kiln co-processing) are emerging technologies. Some manufacturers offer take-back programs. The primary sustainability argument for FRP is its extreme durability (50-100+ year lifespan) that reduces total material consumption. Thermoplastic pultrusion is an emerging alternative offering full recyclability.

**Q32: What maximum span lengths are practical for FRP beams in buildings?**
A: Due to lower stiffness compared to steel, FRP beam spans are typically limited by deflection (L/250 to L/360) rather than strength. Practical spans are 3-6 meters for standard I-beams (150-305 mm deep) under typical floor loads. Hybrid FRP-concrete construction can extend spans to 8-12 meters. For roof structures and pedestrian bridges, all-FRP spans of 10-15 meters are achievable with deep sections or truss configurations. The Aberfeldy bridge spans 63 meters using cable-stayed FRP.

**Q33: Can pultruded FRP profiles be curved or formed into arches?**
A: Pultruded profiles cannot be bent, rolled, or pressed after manufacturing because the thermoset resin is permanently cross-linked (unlike steel). Curved geometries require either segmented straight sections joined at angles, custom-curved pultrusion (limited radius, specialized equipment), or separate manufacturing processes like filament winding or hand layup. For gentle curves, straight profiles 0.5-1.0 m long can be joined to approximate a curve.

**Q34: What acoustic properties do FRP profiles have?**
A: FRP has inherently higher vibration damping than steel (loss factor of 0.01-0.03 vs. 0.001-0.003 for steel), reducing transmitted noise. FRP walkways and platforms are noticeably quieter underfoot than steel grating. However, thin-walled FRP panels can act as sound membranes if not properly stiffened. For noise barrier applications, FRP sandwich panels with foam or mineral wool cores achieve STC ratings of 25-35. The non-resonant behavior of FRP makes it preferred for vibration-sensitive equipment platforms.

**Q35: What dimensional tolerances can architects expect from pultruded profiles?**
A: Standard tolerances per EN 13706-2 and ASTM D3917: cross-section dimensions +/-0.5 mm for profiles up to 100 mm, +/-1% for larger profiles; straightness 1.5 mm per meter; twist 2 degrees per meter length. These are comparable to hot-rolled steel tolerances. Tighter tolerances are achievable with post-machining. Length tolerance is typically +/-3 mm for cut pieces. Profile-to-profile consistency is excellent due to the continuous pultrusion process.

**Q36: How does FRP compare to aluminum for architectural facade applications?**
A: FRP offers superior corrosion resistance (no anodizing needed), 30% lower weight, thermal break properties (thermal conductivity 500x lower than aluminum), and color-through construction. Aluminum has higher stiffness (70 GPa vs. 23-30 GPa), is fully recyclable, and has more established supply chains. FRP is preferred where corrosion resistance, electrical insulation, or thermal performance is critical. Both materials are suitable for unitized curtain wall framing systems.

**Q37: Can FRP profiles achieve a Class A or Class O fire rating for interior use?**
A: Yes, with fire-retardant (FR) resin systems. FR polyester can achieve Class A per ASTM E84 (FSI less than 25, SDI less than 450) and Class 0 per BS 476. Phenolic resin provides the best fire performance with very low smoke and toxicity, suitable for rail and tunnel applications (BS 6853, EN 45545). However, fire resistance ratings (REI 30/60/90 per EN 13501-2) for structural load-bearing require intumescent protection or fire-resistant enclosures, as the resin softens above its Tg.

**Q38: What are the transparency/translucency options for FRP profiles?**
A: Standard pultruded profiles are opaque. Translucent FRP panels (flat or corrugated) are manufactured by separate processes using glass-mat-reinforced polyester, achieving 50-80% light transmission. For architectural daylighting, FRP skylight panels are available per ASTM D3841. Pultruded framing members remain opaque but can be combined with translucent infill panels. Some manufacturers offer semi-translucent thin-walled pultruded sections for specialty applications.

**Q39: How do architects detail FRP-to-FRP and FRP-to-other-material connections?**
A: FRP-to-FRP connections use stainless steel bolts with large-area washers, clip angles, or adhesive bonding (epoxy or methacrylate). FRP-to-steel/concrete connections use standard bolts through pre-drilled holes, with isolation washers to prevent galvanic effects. Concealed connections using internal steel plates or clip systems are available for clean architectural lines. Thermal movement must be accommodated with slotted holes on long runs. Fiberline and Strongwell publish detail libraries for common architectural connections.

**Q40: What is the weight of FRP profiles compared to equivalent steel sections?**
A: Pultruded GFRP has a density of 1.7-2.0 g/cm3, approximately 75-80% lighter than steel (7.85 g/cm3) and 30% lighter than aluminum (2.7 g/cm3). A 200x100 mm FRP I-beam weighs approximately 3.5 kg/m versus 17 kg/m for an equivalent steel section. This enables manual handling without cranes for most profiles, reduces structural dead load, and lowers transportation costs. For a typical platform structure, FRP reduces total structural weight by 60-70%.

## Procurement / Traders (20 Questions)

**Q41: What is the typical price range for pultruded FRP profiles?**
A: Standard GFRP profiles (polyester resin) range from $3-12/kg or $5-25/linear meter depending on profile size and complexity. I-beams and channels cost roughly 2-3x the equivalent steel section by weight but 1.5-2x by linear meter. Vinylester and fire-retardant resins add 15-30% premium. Custom profiles with low volumes can be 3-5x standard pricing. For high-volume infrastructure projects (20,000+ meters), pricing can reach as low as $0.90/meter for simple shapes.

**Q42: What is the typical minimum order quantity (MOQ)?**
A: MOQs vary significantly by supplier: stock profiles from catalog are available in single pieces from distributors. Production runs from manufacturers typically start at 200-500 meters for standard profiles and 500-2,000 meters for custom profiles. Some Chinese suppliers offer MOQs as low as 10-100 meters for testing. European manufacturers (Fiberline, Exel) typically require larger commitments for custom colors or resins. Stock items from Strongwell and Bedford are available with no minimum.

**Q43: What is the typical lead time for pultruded FRP profiles?**
A: Stock profiles: 1-2 weeks (ex-works). Standard profiles in custom lengths: 3-4 weeks. Custom colors on standard shapes: 4-6 weeks. New custom die profiles: 8-16 weeks (including 6-10 weeks for tooling). Fire-retardant or special resin formulations add 1-2 weeks. Rush orders may be available at a premium (15-25% surcharge). Prioritize suppliers with 4 weeks or less lead time and greater than 95% on-time delivery rate.

**Q44: What certifications should I require from FRP suppliers?**
A: Essential: EN 13706-1/2/3 compliance certificate (Europe), ISO 9001 quality management, and material test reports per relevant ASTM standards. Industry-specific: fire test reports (ASTM E84, BS 476), NORSOK M-501 for offshore, FM Approved for industrial safety, DNV/Lloyd's type approval for marine. Third-party testing by accredited labs (e.g., SGS, Bureau Veritas) provides independent verification. Always request batch-specific test certificates, not just type-approval documents.

**Q45: How are pultruded FRP profiles shipped and what packaging is standard?**
A: Profiles are shipped in bundles wrapped with protective film, on timber pallets or in cardboard packaging. Standard lengths are 6 or 12 meters; longer lengths up to 20+ meters are available by flatbed truck. Container shipping for export uses 20-ft or 40-ft containers (maximum profile length 5.9 m or 11.8 m respectively). Profiles should be stored flat on level supports at maximum 1.5 m intervals. Protect from point loads during transport. Freight cost is lower than steel per unit due to lighter weight.

**Q46: What warranty terms do FRP manufacturers typically offer?**
A: Standard product warranties range from 5-10 years covering manufacturing defects and conformance to published specifications. Fiberline offers a 20-year structural warranty. Warranties typically exclude damage from improper installation, chemical exposure beyond published limits, UV degradation on non-UV-stabilized products, and temperatures exceeding the rated Tg. Extended warranties (15-25 years) are available for infrastructure projects with specified maintenance protocols.

**Q47: Can I get free samples before placing an order?**
A: Most manufacturers provide free cut samples (15-30 cm lengths) of standard profiles for material evaluation. Full-length samples (1-2 pieces) for trial fabrication may be provided at cost or free for qualified large projects. Sample lead time is typically 1-2 weeks. Request samples with the specific resin type, color, and surface finish intended for the project. Material test certificates should accompany samples.

**Q48: What payment terms are standard in the FRP industry?**
A: Standard terms: 30% deposit with order confirmation, 70% balance before shipment (for new customers). Established accounts: Net 30 or Net 60 days. Custom tooling requires 50-100% prepayment. Letters of credit (L/C) are common for international orders. Most European and North American manufacturers accept bank transfer, credit card for small orders, and L/C for export. Chinese suppliers typically require T/T with 30% advance.

**Q49: What is the country of origin for the main FRP profile brands?**
A: Strongwell and Bedford: USA. Fiberline: Denmark (now part of Fiberline Building Profiles/Krafton). Exel Composites: Finland (with plants in Austria, Belgium, China, India, Australia). Creative Composites Group (Pultex): Northern Ireland/USA. Topglass: Italy. Wagners CFT: Australia. Krempel: Germany. Liberty Pultrusions: USA. Evergrip: Australia. Rochling: Germany (global manufacturing). Significant manufacturing capacity also exists in China, India, and Turkey.

**Q50: Are there import tariffs or duties on FRP profiles?**
A: FRP profiles are classified under HS code 3926.90 (articles of plastics) or 7019 (glass fiber articles), depending on the country. EU tariff on FRP from China is typically 6.5%. US tariffs on Chinese FRP range from 0-25% depending on classification and current trade policy. No anti-dumping duties currently exist specifically for pultruded FRP profiles (unlike steel). Always verify the current HS classification with your customs broker, as classification disputes are common with composite products.

**Q51: What volume discount structures are typical?**
A: Standard discount tiers: 5-10% for orders exceeding 5,000 kg, 10-15% for orders above 10,000 kg, and 15-25% for annual framework agreements exceeding 50,000 kg. Custom profile pricing drops 20-40% as volumes increase from MOQ to full production run (10,000+ meters). Long-term supply agreements (1-3 years) with quarterly call-offs typically yield the best pricing. Some manufacturers offer project-based pricing for large infrastructure contracts.

**Q52: How do I compare quotations from different FRP suppliers?**
A: Normalize pricing to cost per linear meter for the same profile designation. Verify: resin type (polyester vs. vinylester -- 15-30% price difference), glass content (by weight), compliance with EN 13706 grade (E17 vs. E23), fire rating, UV protection, and surface finish. Request material property data sheets for direct comparison. Include delivered cost (freight significantly varies by origin). Factor in lead time, warranty terms, and technical support capability. Lowest unit price is rarely the best value.

**Q53: Can FRP profiles be sourced with a CE marking?**
A: CE marking for pultruded FRP profiles is not governed by a harmonized European standard for structural products (unlike steel under EN 1090). However, EN 13706 compliance is the accepted quality benchmark. Some manufacturers voluntarily apply CE marking under the Construction Products Regulation based on European Technical Assessments (ETAs). For specific applications (e.g., bridges), a project-specific ETA may be required. Always clarify the legal basis of any CE marking with the supplier.

**Q54: What documentation should accompany an FRP profile delivery?**
A: Minimum documentation: certificate of conformity to specified standard (EN 13706 or equivalent), material test report with actual batch mechanical properties, dimensional inspection report, material safety data sheet (MSDS/SDS), and packing list. Additional: fire test report, UV weathering test data, chemical resistance guide, installation manual, and warranty certificate. For infrastructure projects, third-party inspection reports from accredited laboratories are recommended.

**Q55: How does FRP pricing compare to stainless steel and hot-dip galvanized steel?**
A: By material cost per linear meter, FRP is typically 0.8-1.2x the cost of stainless steel (304/316) and 1.5-2.5x hot-dip galvanized carbon steel. By installed cost (including welding, bolting, surface treatment), FRP is 0.7-1.0x stainless steel and 1.0-1.5x galvanized steel. By 25-year lifecycle cost in corrosive environments, FRP is typically 50-70% of stainless steel and 60-80% of galvanized steel (due to zero maintenance and no recoating costs).

**Q56: Is FRP profile supply stable or are there shortage risks?**
A: Raw material inputs (E-glass fiber and polyester/vinylester resin) are commodity chemicals with multiple global suppliers, so catastrophic supply disruption is unlikely. However, specialty resins (polyurethane, fire-retardant) and certain glass fiber types (ECR-glass) have more limited supply chains. COVID-era resin shortages caused 12-20 week lead times in 2021-2022, now normalized. Maintaining 2-3 qualified suppliers and holding 4-6 weeks of buffer stock is recommended for critical projects.

**Q57: Can FRP profiles be cut to custom lengths before delivery?**
A: Yes, most manufacturers offer cut-to-length service at nominal cost ($0.50-2.00 per cut). Pre-drilled and pre-fabricated profiles are also available from manufacturers with in-house fabrication shops (Strongwell, Creative Composites, Fiberline). CNC machining for complex fabrication adds 15-30% to profile cost but eliminates site dust and waste. Pre-fabrication is recommended for projects where site cutting is restricted (e.g., food processing, clean rooms).

**Q58: What are the Incoterms options typically offered by FRP manufacturers?**
A: Most manufacturers offer: EXW (Ex Works -- buyer arranges all freight), FCA (Free Carrier -- loaded at factory), and DAP (Delivered at Place). FOB and CIF are common for sea freight exports from Chinese manufacturers. European manufacturers commonly quote DDP (Delivered Duty Paid) for intra-EU deliveries. Always clarify insurance responsibility and specify minimum coverage of 110% invoice value for transit. FRP profiles are classified as non-hazardous for transport.

**Q59: Can I private-label or rebrand FRP profiles from a manufacturer?**
A: Yes, many manufacturers offer OEM/private-label arrangements, especially for distributors with annual volume commitments. This includes custom packaging, documentation, and profile marking. Minimum commitment is typically 10,000-50,000 kg/year. The manufacturer provides the test certificates under the private label. Some manufacturers require exclusivity by region. This is common practice in the FRP industry -- several well-known brands source from third-party pultruders.

**Q60: What insurance coverage is recommended when specifying FRP?**
A: Ensure the manufacturer carries product liability insurance of at least $5-10 million. For structural applications, require the manufacturer to provide professional indemnity insurance for their design calculations. Specify that the manufacturer's ISO 9001 quality system is current and audited. For large projects, request a project-specific quality plan and independent third-party inspection. Building owners should confirm that FRP construction is covered by their property insurance policy without exclusions or surcharges.

## Installers / Contractors (20 Questions)

**Q61: What tools are needed to cut pultruded FRP profiles on site?**
A: Use a circular saw with a diamond or carbide-grit blade (not a standard wood or metal blade, which dull rapidly). A worm-gear circular saw provides the best control. For smaller profiles, a reciprocating saw with a carbide-grit blade works. Abrasive cut-off wheels on angle grinders are suitable for quick cuts. Always use slow to moderate feed rates with steady pressure. Avoid high-speed steel (HSS) blades -- fiberglass is extremely abrasive and destroys them within minutes.

**Q62: What PPE is required when cutting and drilling FRP?**
A: Mandatory: safety glasses or goggles (EN 166/ANSI Z87.1), dust mask or P2/N95 respirator (FRP dust is an irritant, not toxic), cut-resistant gloves, long-sleeved shirt or overalls (fiberglass splinters cause skin irritation), and steel-toed boots. For extended cutting operations, use powered dust extraction at the source. FRP dust is non-carcinogenic but is classified as a nuisance particulate. Work in well-ventilated areas. Wash exposed skin with soap and cold water (warm water opens pores).

**Q63: How do I drill holes in pultruded FRP without delamination?**
A: Use sharp carbide-tipped or brad-point drill bits. Drill speed: 300-500 RPM with steady, moderate feed pressure. Always support the back face with a sacrificial board to prevent exit delamination. Minimum edge distance from hole center to edge: 2x hole diameter (4x bolt diameter for structural bolts). Do not use punches -- always drill. Pre-mark all holes before drilling. Clean swarf from holes with compressed air. Hole diameter tolerance should be +0.8 mm to +1.6 mm oversized relative to the bolt.

**Q64: What type of fasteners should be used with FRP profiles?**
A: Stainless steel (A2/A4) bolts, nuts, and washers are standard. Use large-diameter washers (OD at least 2x bolt diameter) to distribute bearing stress and prevent pull-through. Torque to 20-30 Nm for M10-M12 bolts -- do not overtighten, as this crushes the resin. Nylon-insert lock nuts or Nord-Lock washers prevent loosening. Self-tapping screws work for thin sections (less than 6 mm). FRP nuts and bolts are available for fully non-conductive assemblies. Never use plain carbon steel fasteners (galvanic corrosion risk).

**Q65: Can FRP profiles be bonded with adhesives instead of bolted?**
A: Yes. Two-part structural epoxy or methacrylate adhesives provide excellent bond strength (lap shear 10-15 MPa). Surface preparation is critical: sand with 80-grit abrasive, clean with acetone/isopropanol, and bond within 1 hour of preparation. Adhesive joints are stiffer and more efficient than bolted joints (no stress concentration at holes). Cure times: 24 hours at room temperature or 2-4 hours at 60 deg C. Hybrid bolted-bonded joints provide both immediate strength (bolts) and long-term performance (adhesive).

**Q66: How should FRP profiles be stored on site?**
A: Store horizontally on level supports (timber bearers) at maximum 1.5 m intervals to prevent sagging. Keep off the ground on blocks. Protect from point loads, direct sunlight during extended storage (if not UV-stabilized), and impact from other trades. Stack profiles of similar size together with protective interlayers. Maximum stack height: 1.5 m. Cover with breathable tarpaulin (not sealed plastic, which traps moisture). FRP profiles are not damaged by rain but can collect dirt that affects bonding surfaces.

**Q67: What is the weight of typical FRP profiles -- can they be manually handled?**
A: Most pultruded profiles can be manually handled by 1-2 workers. Examples: 100x50 mm channel = 1.5 kg/m, 200x100 mm I-beam = 3.5 kg/m, 150x150x6 mm box = 3.0 kg/m. A 6 m length of 200 mm I-beam weighs only ~21 kg vs. ~102 kg for an equivalent steel section. Profiles up to 12 m can typically be carried by 2-4 workers without lifting equipment. This drastically reduces crane time and enables installation in access-restricted areas.

**Q68: Can pultruded FRP be welded?**
A: No. Thermoset FRP cannot be welded, heated, or bent. Connections are made by bolting, adhesive bonding, or a combination of both. This is a fundamental difference from steel and aluminum. All joints must be pre-designed and pre-detailed. Field modifications are limited to cutting, drilling, and bolting. This makes accurate pre-engineering and shop drawings essential. The inability to weld is offset by the ease of bolted assembly -- no hot work permits, no fire risk, and no specialized welding skills required.

**Q69: How do I repair damaged FRP profiles on site?**
A: Minor surface damage (scratches, chips): fill with polyester or vinylester putty (color-matched), sand smooth, and topcoat with UV-resistant gel coat. Structural damage (cracks, delamination): clean the area, apply wet layup fiberglass repair patches with compatible resin (vinylester or epoxy), and cure per manufacturer instructions. For critical structural members, replacement is preferred over repair. Strongwell's Fabrication and Repair Manual provides detailed repair procedures. All repairs should be inspected and documented.

**Q70: What are the recommended bolt torque values for FRP connections?**
A: Typical torque values: M8 bolts = 10-15 Nm, M10 = 15-20 Nm, M12 = 20-30 Nm, M16 = 30-45 Nm. These are significantly lower than steel connection torques because FRP is susceptible to through-thickness crushing. Use a calibrated torque wrench. Do not use impact drivers. Tighten in a star pattern for multi-bolt connections. Re-check torque after 24 hours as initial seating occurs. Over-torquing is the most common installation error with FRP -- it causes local crushing and reduces connection capacity.

**Q71: How do I achieve accurate alignment when assembling FRP structures?**
A: Use temporary clamping with C-clamps or bar clamps designed for FRP (no marring clamp faces). Pre-drill pilot holes in jigs for consistent bolt patterns. Use shims (stainless steel or FRP) for alignment adjustment. FRP does not tolerate forced alignment like steel -- forcing causes cracking. All connections should allow +/-2 mm adjustment. Alignment tolerance of 3 mm in 3 m is typical. Use laser levels and string lines for long runs. Assembly sequence should be planned to maintain structural stability.

**Q72: Can I field-cut openings in FRP grating?**
A: Yes. Mark the opening with a marker (not a scriber, which damages fibers). Cut with a carbide-grit or diamond blade on a circular saw or jigsaw. Maintain minimum 25 mm (1 inch) from bearing bars to the edge of the opening. File cut edges smooth and seal with resin sealant (UV-resistant) to prevent moisture wicking into exposed fiber ends. Remove at least two bearing bars of support width on each side of the opening. Follow the manufacturer's span table to verify remaining capacity.

**Q73: How do I control dust when fabricating FRP on site?**
A: Use power tools with integrated dust extraction (vacuum attachment). Wet cutting reduces airborne dust by 80-90% but creates slurry that requires cleanup. Establish a designated cutting area downwind from other workers. Barricade the cutting zone and post warning signs. Use a HEPA-filtered vacuum for cleanup (not compressed air, which re-disperses fibers). FRP dust is classified as a nuisance particulate (OEL: 10 mg/m3 total, 5 mg/m3 respirable per OSHA). Dispose of FRP waste as general construction waste (non-hazardous).

**Q74: How do FRP grating panels connect to the supporting structure?**
A: Use manufacturer-supplied hold-down clips (saddle clips) that clamp over bearing bars and bolt to the support steel. Install clips every 450 mm (18 inches) and at every support point. Maximum gap between bearing bar ends and supporting structure: 6 mm (1/4 inch). For corrosive environments, use FRP or stainless steel clips. Panels are not welded or permanently fixed -- they are removable for access. Leave 3-6 mm gap between adjacent panels for thermal expansion.

**Q75: What is the maximum span for FRP grating without intermediate support?**
A: Span depends on grating type, thickness, and load. For 38 mm thick pultruded grating under 5 kPa uniform load with L/200 deflection limit: approximately 900-1200 mm span. For 50 mm thick: 1200-1500 mm. Molded grating spans are shorter than pultruded for equivalent thickness. Always use the manufacturer's load/span tables, which account for safety factor (typically 1.5x on deflection, 4.0x on ultimate). Check both uniform load and concentrated point load (1 kN on 100x100 mm area per EN ISO 14122-2).

**Q76: Can FRP profiles be installed in winter/cold weather?**
A: Yes. FRP can be installed in temperatures from -40 deg C to +50 deg C. Cold temperatures make FRP slightly more brittle, so handle with care and avoid dropping. Bolted connections are unaffected by temperature. Adhesive bonding should not be performed below 5 deg C (most structural adhesives require minimum 10-15 deg C for proper cure). Storage in freezing conditions is acceptable. FRP does not contract/expand as much as aluminum and is less affected by cold than many materials.

**Q77: How do I install FRP handrails and guardrails?**
A: FRP rail systems use pultruded posts (typically 50x50 mm square tube), top rail, mid rail, and optional kick plate. Posts are bolted to the structure using base plates (4x anchor bolts per post). Standard post spacing: 1200-1500 mm. Top rail height: 1000-1100 mm per EN ISO 14122-3. Rails connect to posts using FRP or stainless steel brackets with bolted connections. System must resist 1.0 kN point load at top rail. Factory-assembled modules speed installation by 40-50% compared to field assembly.

**Q78: What is the expected installation rate for FRP structures compared to steel?**
A: FRP installation is typically 30-50% faster than steel due to: no welding (eliminates hot work permits, fire watch, welding inspection), lighter weight (manual handling, less crane time), pre-fabricated components, and simple bolted connections using standard tools. A typical industrial platform (100 m2) can be installed by a 4-person crew in 2-3 days with FRP vs. 4-6 days with steel. The learning curve for first-time FRP installers is typically 1-2 projects.

**Q79: How do I make field repairs to FRP grating?**
A: For broken or damaged bearing bars, cut out the damaged section and install a replacement panel, leaving 6 mm gap to adjacent panels. For minor cracks in bearing bars, apply a fiberglass repair patch with vinylester resin. For damaged cross rods, replace individual rods by drilling out the old connections and re-fastening. Anti-slip grit surfaces can be renewed by applying epoxy resin with broadcast aggregate. Always restore the original load path and verify remaining capacity matches design requirements.

**Q80: Can FRP profiles be painted on site?**
A: Yes. Surface preparation: light sand with 120-180 grit, clean with solvent (acetone or MEK), and allow to dry. Apply two-part polyurethane or epoxy paint system. Do not use oil-based paints. Primer is recommended for best adhesion. FRP accepts paint well when properly prepared. However, painting is usually unnecessary because color is integral to the resin. Paint is typically only applied for exact color matching to adjacent steel structures, corporate branding, or to refresh weathered profiles.

## Building Owners / End Users (20 Questions)

**Q81: What is the expected lifespan of pultruded FRP structures?**
A: Pultruded FRP has a proven service life of 30-50 years in typical applications, with many manufacturers citing design lives of 50-100+ years based on accelerated aging tests. Strongwell customers report profiles installed in 1979 still in service. In non-aggressive environments, FRP effectively has an indefinite lifespan because it does not corrode, rot, or suffer fatigue from normal loading. The resin matrix may degrade slightly at the surface from UV exposure, but this does not affect structural performance.

**Q82: What maintenance does FRP require?**
A: FRP requires minimal maintenance compared to steel (no painting, no corrosion protection). Recommended maintenance: visual inspection twice per year for cracks, delamination, surface wear, or loose connections; annual cleaning with low-pressure water and mild detergent; re-torque bolted connections after the first year; replace damaged anti-slip grit surfaces every 5-10 years. No protective coatings, cathodic protection, or ongoing surface treatment is required. Total annual maintenance cost is typically less than 1% of installed value.

**Q83: How do I clean FRP structures?**
A: Low-pressure water washing (garden hose pressure, not pressure washer above 1500 psi) with a mild non-abrasive detergent (pH 6-9). For stubborn stains, use a soft-bristle brush. Avoid abrasive cleaners, scouring pads, or solvents that could damage the surface veil. For grease removal, use a citrus-based cleaner or diluted isopropanol. In food processing environments, FRP is compatible with standard sanitizing agents (quaternary ammonium, sodium hypochlorite up to 10%). Clean grating with a stiff broom to remove debris from between bars.

**Q84: Can FRP profiles be painted or re-finished if the appearance fades?**
A: Yes. Lightly sand with 120-180 grit sandpaper, wipe clean with solvent, and apply a two-part polyurethane topcoat. This restores color and UV protection for 10-15 years. Alternatively, a UV-protective clear coat can be applied without changing color. Surface chalking (white powder on aged profiles) is cosmetic only and does not indicate structural degradation. In most outdoor applications, periodic cleaning alone is sufficient to maintain acceptable appearance.

**Q85: Is FRP safe for use in food processing and potable water facilities?**
A: Yes. FRP with FDA-approved (21 CFR 177.2420) or NSF/ANSI 61 certified resin systems is approved for contact with potable water and food processing environments. The smooth, non-porous surface resists bacterial growth and is easy to sanitize. FRP does not leach chemicals in normal service conditions. It is widely used in water treatment plants, food factories, and pharmaceutical facilities. Always specify the appropriate food-grade resin system and request the compliance certificate.

**Q86: Does FRP conduct electricity? Is it safe for use near electrical equipment?**
A: No. Pultruded GFRP is electrically non-conductive, with a dielectric strength of 15-45 kV/mm. This makes it inherently safe for use near electrical equipment, eliminating shock hazards and eliminating the need for grounding. FRP is widely used for substation structures, cable trays, and utility pole crossarms. However, carbon fiber reinforced polymer (CFRP) IS electrically conductive and should not be confused with GFRP. Note: in explosive atmospheres, anti-static FRP formulations are required (surface resistance less than 10^6 ohm).

**Q87: How does FRP perform in extreme heat or fire?**
A: In normal service, FRP handles temperatures up to 65-100 deg C continuously (depending on resin). In fire, FRP will char and lose structural capacity but does not melt, drip, or propagate flame with fire-retardant resins. Fire-retardant FRP self-extinguishes when the flame source is removed. Smoke density is a concern -- specify low-smoke resins (fire-retardant or intumescent-coated) for enclosed spaces. FRP structures in fire zones should be designed with appropriate fire detection and suppression systems.

**Q88: Will FRP fade or discolor in sunlight?**
A: UV-stabilized FRP with surface veil retains color for 20-30 years in outdoor exposure. Non-UV-stabilized profiles may show noticeable fading and chalking within 5-10 years. Darker colors tend to fade more than lighter colors. Fading is cosmetic only -- structural properties are not affected by surface UV degradation (limited to 0.1-0.3 mm depth). If long-term color retention is critical, specify RAL-matched UV-stable resin or apply a periodic (every 10-15 years) UV-protective topcoat.

**Q89: Is FRP resistant to vandalism, graffiti, and intentional damage?**
A: FRP is more vandal-resistant than timber and comparable to steel for common damage types. It cannot be bent or pried apart like aluminum. Graffiti can be removed with appropriate solvents without damaging the FRP surface (test solvent compatibility in an inconspicuous area first). However, FRP can be damaged by heavy impacts with sharp objects (unlike steel, which dents but maintains integrity). Anti-graffiti coatings are available and compatible with FRP surfaces.

**Q90: What happens if FRP is exposed to chemicals or solvents accidentally?**
A: Standard polyester FRP resists most common chemicals: mild acids and alkalis, saltwater, petroleum fuels, and cleaning agents. It should not be exposed to strong oxidizing agents, concentrated alkalis (above pH 14), acetone, methylene chloride, or hot concentrated acids for prolonged periods. Vinylester FRP offers broader chemical resistance. If an accidental spill occurs, flush with water immediately and inspect for surface softening, discoloration, or blistering. Consult the manufacturer's chemical resistance chart for specific chemical compatibility.

**Q91: Does FRP require any insurance or regulatory compliance considerations for building owners?**
A: FRP structures should be covered under standard building insurance policies. Notify your insurer that FRP is being used, as some policies may have exclusions for non-traditional building materials. Fire rating documentation (ASTM E84, BS 476 test reports) should be retained for insurance and compliance records. Building code compliance documentation from the structural engineer is essential. In most jurisdictions, FRP is accepted under performance-based design provisions where prescriptive codes do not specifically address it.

**Q92: Can FRP structures be modified or expanded after initial installation?**
A: Yes. Bolted FRP structures can be disassembled, modified, and reassembled with standard tools. New profiles can be bolted to existing ones. Additional grating panels can be added alongside existing panels. The non-welded, bolted construction makes FRP structures inherently more flexible for future modification than welded steel. Components can be reused in new configurations. Keep spare profiles and connection hardware in stock for future modifications.

**Q93: How does FRP handle freeze-thaw cycles?**
A: FRP is unaffected by freeze-thaw cycling because it does not absorb water (water absorption less than 1% per ASTM D570). This is a major advantage over concrete (spalling from freeze-thaw) and wood (rot from moisture). FRP has been proven in Arctic environments (Scandinavia, Canada, Alaska) with decades of service. The low CTE prevents thermal stress cracking. No winterization or seasonal protection is required.

**Q94: What is the noise level of FRP walkways and platforms compared to steel?**
A: FRP walkways generate noticeably less impact noise than steel grating due to FRP's higher material damping coefficient (10-20x that of steel). Footfall noise is reduced by approximately 10-15 dB compared to open steel grating. Solid FRP planks are quieter than open-bar grating. For noise-sensitive environments (hospitals, residential areas, nighttime operations), FRP walkways with solid treads provide the quietest option. Rubber anti-vibration pads under supports further reduce transmitted noise.

**Q95: Can FRP structures support heavy equipment or vehicle loads?**
A: Yes, with proper design. Pultruded FRP beams can be designed for equipment loads exceeding 50 kN. FRP bridge decks carry highway vehicle loads (HL-93 per AASHTO or Load Model 1 per EN 1991-2). FRP grating is available rated for forklift traffic. The key is proper span design and adequate bearing support. For concentrated heavy loads, use load distribution plates and verify web crippling capacity. Always have the structural engineer check the specific loading against the manufacturer's design tables.

**Q96: What is the environmental impact of FRP compared to steel and timber?**
A: FRP has a lower operational carbon footprint than steel due to zero maintenance, lighter transport weight, and no recoating. Manufacturing CO2 is 60-70% lower per ton than steel. However, end-of-life disposal is less favorable than steel (which is nearly 100% recyclable). Compared to treated timber, FRP eliminates chemical preservative leaching and deforestation concerns. The total lifecycle environmental impact favors FRP in long-service applications (more than 20 years) where steel would require multiple recoating or replacement cycles.

**Q97: Can FRP be used in swimming pools, water parks, and high-humidity environments?**
A: FRP is ideal for aquatic environments. It is impervious to chlorinated water, resistant to mold and algae growth, does not corrode from humidity, and provides anti-slip walking surfaces. FRP grating and structural profiles are widely used in water treatment plants, swimming pool decks, and water park structures. The non-conductive property is a safety advantage in wet environments. Specify vinylester resin for prolonged immersion and elevated chlorine levels above 5 ppm.

**Q98: How do I dispose of FRP at end of life?**
A: FRP is classified as non-hazardous waste and can be disposed of in standard construction and demolition landfills. Mechanical recycling (grinding into filler for cement or new composites) is available through specialist recyclers. Thermal recycling via cement kiln co-processing recovers energy and incorporates glass fibers into clinker. Chemical recycling is emerging but not yet commercially widespread. Some manufacturers offer take-back programs. Contact your waste management provider for local disposal options. FRP contains no heavy metals or toxic leachates.

**Q99: Does FRP attract or support mold, mildew, or biological growth?**
A: No. FRP is non-porous and does not absorb moisture, so it does not support mold, mildew, fungi, or insect infestation. This is a significant advantage over wood and even some metals (which develop biofilms in damp conditions). In marine environments, FRP resists barnacle attachment better than bare steel (though biofouling can occur on any submerged surface). For hygienic environments, FRP's smooth surface is easy to clean and sanitize to GMP standards.

**Q100: What happens to FRP in a lightning strike or strong electromagnetic field?**
A: GFRP is electromagnetically transparent -- it does not attract lightning, does not interfere with radio/radar signals, and is not affected by electromagnetic fields. This makes it ideal for antenna towers, radar platforms, and MRI room structures. In lightning-prone areas, FRP structures require separate lightning protection systems (conductors and grounding) because FRP cannot serve as a natural conductor. Carbon FRP (CFRP) does conduct electricity and behaves differently -- ensure you specify GFRP for non-conductive applications.
